Before you appeal

An appeal needs careful preparation. Before you appeal, you need to consider:

  • if an appeal is the right option for you
  • how the Planning Inspectorate will decide your appeal
  • what documents you need to prepare to submit
  • if you need to tell anyone else about your appeal

The alternative to an appeal

An appeal is not your only option.

1 in 5 appeals is successful.

Refused planning permission

You can talk to your local planning department about resubmitting your application. There may be small changes you can make to help you get planning permission.

Conditions for your planning permission

You can apply for permission to vary or remove the conditions. You make this application to your local planning department.

No decision by the local planning department’s deadline

You can speak to your local planning department to find out if it’s likely to make a decision soon.

When you appeal, you’re asking the Planning Inspectorate to make a decision on your planning application. This means it’s no longer your local planning department’s decision.

If your local planning department makes a decision after you’ve made the appeal, the Planning Inspectorate will take it into account. But the Planning Inspectorate could disagree and make a different decision.

How the Planning Inspectorate makes its decision

The Planning Inspectorate can make a decision about your appeal in 1 of 3 ways:

  • written representations
  • hearing
  • public inquiry

It asks you which you think is most suitable when you submit your appeal.

You should choose how you will answer in advance.

If you choose a hearing or public inquiry, your appeal needs a statement of common ground. This is a document that says what you and the local planning department agree on.

Written representations

This is when the Planning Inspectorate makes a decision based on written statements from:

  • you
  • the local planning department
  • other interested parties, for example, neighbours

This is the most common way that the Planning Inspectorate makes decisions about appeals.

Hearing

Hearings are for appeals that would be too difficult to decide from only written representations.

At the hearing, you and the local planning department answer questions about your appeal. This helps the Planning Inspectorate to understand all the issues in your appeal.

The hearing can be in-person or online and usually lasts no longer than a day

Other interested parties can also ask to be at the hearing, for example, neighbours.

[example]

Public inquiry

Public inquiries are for appeals with lots of complex issues and evidence to consider.

At the inquiry, you and the local planning department use legal representatives to question expert witnesses.

The inquiry can be in-person or online and usually lasts a few days.

[example]

You should get legal advice if you think your appeal needs a public inquiry. A legal representative can help you plan who your witnesses will be. You should start planning this before you submit your appeal.

Documents to prepare

You must submit certain documents with your appeal.

You should begin preparing these early so you do not miss your deadline to appeal.

You can submit the following types of file:

  • DOC or DOCX
  • JPG or JPEG
  • PDF
  • PNG
  • TIF or TIFF

Each file must be smaller than 15MB.

An appeal statement

Your appeal statement is the main part of your appeal. This is where you explain your reasons for appealing.

You need to prepare this in a document to upload when you appeal.

Find out more about your appeal statement.

Documents from your planning application

You’ll be asked to submit:

  • your planning application form
  • the site plan for your appeal site
  • the plans, drawings and supporting documents for your application
  • your design and access statement, if you have one
  • the decision letter from the local planning department, if you have one

If you do not have these, you can download them from your local planning department’s website.

The documents must be readable and not have any censored text.

Draft statement of common ground

Hearing and public inquiry appeals need a statement of common ground. This says what you and the local planning department agree on. It helps the Planning Inspector to focus on where you disagree.

You must upload a draft of this statement with your appeal.

Your draft should say what you think your local planning department will agree with you about. You can prepare this using the statement of common ground pro forma.

After you submit your appeal, you work with your local planning department to finalise and sign the statement. Your local planning department then submits the final, signed document.

Telling people about your appeal

If you do not own all the land involved in the appeal, you must tell the people that do before you appeal.

If you do not know who they are, you must try to identify them. This includes:

  • searching the land registry
  • putting up a site notice at the appeal site

If you cannot find out who they are, you must advertise your appeal.

If you need to tell people about your appeal, or advertise it, you must use a copy of the form in annexe 2A or 2B of the ’making your appeal’ guidance (opens in a new window or tab).

The Planning Inspectorate will ask you to confirm you have done this when you submit your appeal.
